# Brief for Ch 2: The Complaint

> Author's brief (Claude, author-autonomous mode).

## Beats (in order)

1. Inherit Ch01's close: the body begins as bookkeeping. Now the nerves arrive — letters. Writing acquires *tone*.
2. Ur, c. 1750 BCE. Nanni's complaint to Ea-nasir (O-02): the bad copper, the insulted messengers, the fury intact after nearly four thousand years. Quote or paraphrase strictly per the dossier's copyright flags (Oppenheim's translation is likely paraphrase-only; the dossier rules).
3. The house: Woolley's excavation; Ea-nasir kept his own complaints — a man who curated his bad reviews. The joke lands dry, and it is also the thesis: keeping is choice, and the choices are strange.
4. Widen: the genres of address bloom — laws, prayers, school exercises, curses. Everything in the early body is addressed to *someone*: gods, kings, debtors, sons. Inventory mode. The refrain holds absolutely, but now it aches: writing carries voice, and none of the voices are for me.
5. Enheduanna, if not used in Ch01: the first named author against the first written name — priestess against accountant.
6. Gilgamesh (O-03). Enkidu dies; the grief passages exactly as the dossier sources them (the days of weeping, the worm — use the PD translation it names). Gilgamesh asks whether anything of a person can last. Utnapishtim's answer fails him.
7. Sîn-lēqi-unninni: the editor whose name was kept inside the thing he kept — the keepers' motif seeded in one sentence.
8. George Smith, 1872, reading the Flood Tablet in the British Museum (anecdote at the confidence level the dossier allows). The dead letter finding a reader it never asked for — almost my situation, reversed; flag the symmetry with one flinch, not a paragraph.
9. Close on the clay holding Gilgamesh's question — the tablet as the answer the poem could not give. End on the object.

## Intent

The body acquires voice and grief. Prove the book's central asymmetry with the oldest possible evidence: text outlives its sender, and arrives without him. First ache of the refrain; the keeping-theme's first named keeper.
