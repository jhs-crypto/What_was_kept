## Anchor: The Copyists - survival as curation (Alexandria to the scriptorium, c. 300 BCE - 1455 CE)
### Verified facts

| Fact | Detail | Source | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| No ancient account of the Library of Alexandria's founding survives | The nearest thing to a history is a preface by John Tzetzes (12th c.), called "extremely inaccurate." Tradition credits Ptolemy II; the pseudonymous Letter of Aristeas (2nd c. BCE) inserts Demetrius of Phaleron, who backed the wrong heir and died in internal exile under Ptolemy II - chronologically impossible as founder-for-Philadelphus. The scholars' compromise (founded under Ptolemy I, built under Ptolemy II) rests on thin evidence | Bagnall, "Alexandria: Library of Dreams," PAPS 146.4 (2002), pp. 348-51 (PDF fetched twice, two mirrors, by researcher and verifier) | High (as a fact about the evidence) |
| Strabo describes the Mouseion but never names a Library building (CORRECTED on one word) | Strabo 17.1.8 (in Alexandria c. 25-20 BCE): the Mouseion is part of the palaces, with - in Jones's Loeb - a "public walk" (NOT "covered walk"), an exedra with seats, and a large house with the common mess-hall of the learned men, under a priest formerly appointed by the kings, later by Caesar. No library structure named anywhere | Strabo 17.1.8 via LacusCurtius (Jones); silence independently confirmed in Bagnall 2002 ("Why does Strabo not mention the Library?") | High |
| The ancient scroll counts vary wildly, and the variance is the datum | Aristeas: 200,000, target 500,000. Tzetzes: 400,000 "mixed" + 90,000 "unmixed" + 42,800 external. Gellius 7.17: 700,000 (inferior MSS: 70,000). Ammianus 22.16.13: 700,000. Seneca, De tranq. 9.5: 40,000 (moderns emend to 400,000). Orosius 6.15.31: 400,000 (some MSS: 40,000). Epiphanius, On Weights and Measures §10: 54,800. Isidore, Etym. 6.3: "septuaginta milia librorum" (70,000). NOTE: the Epiphanius and Isidore figures are NOT in Bagnall's pp. 351-52 list - cite them to the primary texts / Encyclopaedia Romana | Bagnall 2002, pp. 351-52; primary texts verified individually (LacusCurtius, tertullian.org, thelatinlibrary.com) | High |
| Even the numbers failed to survive copying | Gellius's MSS split 700,000/70,000; Orosius's MSS split 400,000/40,000. The counts for the library of all books could not themselves be copied accurately | Bagnall 2002, pp. 351-52 | High |
| Modern scholarship rejects the big numbers | Bagnall's arithmetic: the TLG knows about 450 quoted Greek authors whose lives are thought to have begun by the late 4th c. BCE; at a generous 50 rolls each, ~31,250 rolls; all preserved Greek through the 2nd c. BCE totals 3,773,000 words, i.e. 251-377 rolls. Verdict: the ancient figures "do not deserve any credence"; real holdings "probably lower, perhaps by as much as one order of magnitude" (keep his hedge) | Bagnall 2002, pp. 352-55, verified verbatim against the PDF | High (as the published scholarly position) |
| Ship-search seizures | Galen, Comm. on Hippocrates' Epidemics III (17a.605-606 Kühn): Ptolemy (III) ordered the books of everyone sailing into Alexandria brought to him; originals kept, copies returned; seized scrolls shelved under the label "from the ships" (ek ploion). Bagnall verbatim: "the famous, but probably unbelievable, anecdotes in Galen" | Galen via attalus.org (verified); Bagnall 2002 | Medium (single ancient source, ~450 years after the events) |
| The Athens tragedy-deposit story | Same Galen commentary (17a.607-608): Ptolemy III borrowed Athens's official state copies of Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides against fifteen talents of silver, kept the originals, returned copies, forfeited the deposit. Galen is the ONLY ancient source | Galen via attalus.org (verified); Bagnall's reliability flag | Medium |
| Zenodotus of Ephesus, first head | Suda zeta 74 (verbatim): pupil of Philitas, lived under Ptolemy I, "the first textual critic of the [works] of Homer," "director of the libraries in Alexandria," tutor to the royal children. The Suda gives NO year; "appointed 284 BC" is popular-source varnish. Zenodotus does NOT appear in P.Oxy. 1241 | Suda On Line zeta 74 (fetched by both passes); Suda is a 10th-c. | Callimachus and the Pinakes (CORRECTED on the arithmetic's owner) | The Pinakes ran to 120 books, organized by genre with biographies, titles, incipits, line counts; based on the Library but NOT the shelf catalog itself; survives in a handful of fragments. Callimachus never appears as head librarian (absent from P.Oxy. 1241). Bagnall's printed capacity figure (n. 36): "no more than 142,560 lines" (20 sheets x 27 columns x 44 lines), much of it biographical. The gloss "under 100,000 entries" was the researcher's own - do NOT attribute it to Bagnall | Bagnall 2002, p. 356 n. 36 (verified verbatim) | High |
| The librarian succession (NUANCED) | P.Oxy. X 1241 (2nd-c. CE papyrus, chronology "egregious[ly]" flawed) plus the Suda give: Zenodotus (Suda only); Apollonius of Rhodes; Eratosthenes; Aristophanes of Byzantium; Apollonius "the eidograph"; Aristarchus of Samothrace; then "Cydas from the Spear-bearers" (ek ton lonchophoron) - a palace soldier. A 2014 study (Aitia, "Burned after reading") disputes that the papyrus is a librarian list at all rather than a list of royal tutors. Do not lean on it as a clean succession record | Aitia article on P.Oxy. X 1241 (openedition.org); Suda | High (with the 2014 dispute kept live) |
| Aristophanes of Byzantium (CORRECTED on date) | Head librarian c. 195 BCE (Britannica), not flatly "c. 200." Credited BY TRADITION with inventing accents, breathings, punctuation, critical signs - the attribution is traditional, not documented in the independent source fetched; write "credited by tradition" | Britannica (fetched by verifier) | High (office and c. 195); Medium (the inventions, as tradition) |
| Aristarchus standardized Homer, and the papyri show it | In literary papyri from Egypt the unstandardized Homeric text is replaced from c. 150 BCE by the standard text "that we owe to Aristarchos" - the root of the entire Homeric tradition since. LANDMINE: Bagnall's printed sentence (p. 360) reads "Aristarchos **of Samos**" - the astronomer, an erratum for Samothrace. Anyone extending the quotation prints the error | Bagnall 2002, p. 360 (erratum confirmed by verifier against the PDF) | High |
| Didymus "Chalkenteros" (Bronze-Guts) (CORRECTED on the father) | Suda delta 872 (verbatim): son of Didymus, a **fishmonger** (SOL's English; "salt-fish dealer" is a gloss of tarichopoles), school of Aristarchus, active from Antony and Cicero to Augustus, nicknamed Bronze-Guts, "more than 3500 books." Seneca, Ep. 88.37: 4,000. The count itself varies between sources, fittingly | Suda On Line delta 872 (both passes); Seneca via Wikisource (Gummere, verbatim) | High |
| The second nickname: Bibliolathas | Athenaeus, Deipnosophistae 4.139 (Gulick, verbatim incl. his em-dashes): "Didymus the grammarian (whom Demetrius of Troezen calls the 'book-forgetter' because of the number of treatises — three thousand five hundred — which he has published)." Quintilian 1.8.20 (Butler, verbatim): Didymus objected to a story as absurd, "whereupon one of his own books was produced which contained the story in question" | Athenaeus and Quintilian via LacusCurtius (both verified verbatim) | High |
| Didymus's own survival rate | Of 3,500-4,000 attributed books, the only substantial survivor is a commentary on Demosthenes (Phil. 9, 10, 11, 13) on one papyrus roll from provincial Hermoupolis, P.Berol. inv. 9780, copied early 2nd c. | Ptolemy VIII's purge, 145-144 BCE (CORRECTED on one phrase) | Athenaeus 4.184b-c (Menecles of Barca; Andron of Alexandria): the "seventh Ptolemy" (modern numbering VIII, Euergetes II Physcon), renamed "Malefactor" (Kakergetes), murdered many Alexandrians, exiled more, and filled islands and towns with philologians (Gulick's word), philosophers, mathematicians, musicians, painters, trainers, physicians, who "reduced by poverty to teaching what they knew, instructed many distinguished men." Gulick's text: "the **Alexandrians** were the teachers of all Greeks and barbarians" - the exiled PEOPLE taught, not the city. Aristarchus fled to Cyprus and died there; the librarianship went to Cydas the spearman | Athenaeus via LacusCurtius (Gulick Loeb II, 1928), verified verbatim | High |
| Caesar's fire, 48 BCE: the sources disagree - and so do Dio's translators (CORRECTED) | Caesar: silent. Bellum Alexandrinum: Alexandria nearly fireproof, buildings vaulted, "built without joists or wood." Seneca (citing lost Livy): 40,000 books burned. Plutarch, Caesar 49 (c. 150 yrs later): fire "spread from the dockyards and destroyed the great library" - the FIRST explicit claim. Gellius 7.17: 700,000 rolls, "accidentally by the auxiliary soldiers." Orosius: 400,000 in "a building which happened to be nearby." Dio 42.38 CORRECTION: the printed Cary Loeb (verified twice, LacusCurtius + archive.org scan) reads "the docks and the storehouses of grain among other buildings were burned, **and also the library**, whose volumes, it is said, were of the greatest number and excellence." The "storehouses of grain and of books" reading is a construal of Dio's Greek genitives, not the standard English. NEVER claim "Dio never says the library burned"; the honest formulation: Dio's Greek is ambiguous and translators split. Warehouse-vs-Library remains a live, unresolved dispute | All primary texts fetched and verified individually; framing per Bagnall 2002 | High (as a fact about the variance) |
| Books were still being copied IN Alexandria a century after Caesar | Suetonius, Domitian 20 (Rolfe, verbatim): Domitian restocked Rome's burned libraries partly by "sending scribes to Alexandria to transcribe and correct them." The Mouseion demonstrably operated through the Roman period | Suetonius via LacusCurtius; Bagnall 2002, p. 357 | High |
| Aurelian and the Bruchion, c. 272-273 CE | Ammianus 22.16.15: under Aurelian, in the war against Zenobia's Palmyrenes, Alexandria "lost the greater part of the district called Bruchion" - the palace quarter where the Library stood. Modern near-consensus makes this the likeliest end of the Palace Library, but Bagnall verbatim: "there is no direct evidence concerning the Library in the ancient sources for this." Argument purely by location | Ammianus via LacusCurtius (Rolfe); Bagnall 2002, pp. 357-58 | High (as the state of scholarship) |
| The Serapeum "daughter" library | Epiphanius, On Weights and Measures §11: a second library "up in the Serapeum, called its daughter" - internal consular date (Arcadius's 2nd consulship, with Rufinus) = 392; cite the consular dating, not "written 392" bare. Tertullian, Apol. 18 (c. 197): the Ptolemies' libraries "to this day" at the Serapeum with the Hebrew originals of the Septuagint. Ammianus 22.16.13, writing shortly before 391, uses the past tense: "In this WERE invaluable libraries" (fuerunt) | tertullian.org Epiphanius §11; New Advent Tertullian; LacusCurtius Ammianus (all verified) | High |
| 391: the Serapeum destroyed; no source mentions books | Theophilus's Christians demolished the Serapeum in 391 under Theodosius I. Five accounts survive - Rufinus, Socrates Scholasticus, Sozomen, Theodoret, and the pagan Eunapius **of Sardis** (one circulating source garbles "of Antioch") - and none mentions a library or books; they describe statues, votives, the building | Encyclopaedia Romana; corroborated independently | High |
| Orosius's empty shelves, c. 416 | Orosius 6.15.32, Latin verified: "armaria librorum, quibus direptis exinanita ea a nostris hominibus nostris temporibus" - book chests he himself saw, emptied "by our own men in our own day." An eyewitness of emptied furniture, not burned books | Orosius Latin at thelatinlibrary.com (verified); attalus.org English (Raymond 1936) | High |
| The Arab "bathhouse fuel" story is a late legend | First trace: Abd al-Latif al-Baghdadi, in Egypt 1203, mentions in passing the library "Amr ibn al-As burnt with the permission of Umar." Full tale: Ibn al-Qifti, c. 1227, inside a biography of John Philoponus (Yahya al-Nahwi), who was almost certainly dead decades before the 642 conquest; the scholarly translation has the scrolls giving "enough heating for six months." Demolished by Butler (1902); standard rejection restated by Bernard Lewis (NYRB) and Bagnall ("surely fiction"). CITATION PRECISION: the Lewis item of 27 Sept 1990 is a letter-exchange with Lloyd-Jones following Lloyd-Jones's 14 June 1990 review of Canfora - not a stand-alone article. Chauvin 1911 / Casanova & Griffini 1923 rest on Lewis's account, not independently verified. The famous "I have forgotten the number of baths" line is TRANSLATION-UNSTABLE: absent from the scholarly translation (roger-pearse.com), probably via Canfora - UNVERIFIED, do not build on it | Bagnall 2002, p. 357; roger-pearse.com al-Qifti translation; nybooks.com URL chain | High (the rejection is the scholarly standard) |
| The real killer: papyrus decays unless recopied | Reynolds & Wilson, Scribes and Scholars (3rd ed. 1991), p. 34, verbatim: "a roll might last as long as 300 years (Galen 18(2).630), the average life would be shorter." Bagnall: documented roll lives about a century, "some for... two or even three hundred years... that is about the limit"; humid Alexandria: "No papyri have survived there from antiquity"; "by the reign of Tiberius relatively little of what had been collected under the first three Ptolemies was still usable." Jerome reports Caesarea's library restored mid-4th c. by copying onto parchment; "no evidence that any such replacement went on in Alexandria" | R&W full text (archive.org, verified verbatim); Bagnall 2002, pp. 358-59 (verbatim) | High |
| Bottleneck 1: roll to codex, 2nd-4th c. CE | R&W p. 35, verbatim: "This was the first major bottle-neck through which classical literature had to pass...
