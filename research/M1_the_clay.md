### Disputed / corrected during verification

- **Englund attribution corrected.** The transliteration "5,617 1/5 N1 ... 37 months ... Kushim's final account" is genuine Englund scholarship and arithmetically consistent with the catalog's 134,813 litres - but the dossier had cited it to Guinness World Records, whose current page contains neither the transliteration nor the figure. Cite Englund (e.g. Nissen, Damerow & Englund, *Archaic Bookkeeping*).
- **The archive breakdown corrected twice over.** The original list omitted the Metropolitan Museum's 3 tablets; and once the Met is added, the Schøyen catalog's own itemization (25+30+4+3+4+6) sums to 72 against its stated total of 77. The catalog disagrees with itself - an accounting error in the description of history's first accountant. Use "roughly 75" or quote with attribution.
- **Seizure timing corrected.** The Økokrim raid was late August 2021; "September 2021" was the date of press coverage. The "75 tablets returned, 16 September 2022" figures come from Schøyen's own announcement and must be attributed as such; the return itself is independently confirmed (Morgenbladet, 23 Sept 2022).
- **Disk year corrected.** 1926 (Penn Museum object record; Morgan catalog), not the popularly repeated 1927.
- **Civil's "40%" ruled unverifiable and dropped.** The authorship dispute stands perfectly well without it: even skeptics give the book its richest version of the fact - if the attribution is a later construction, "the first author" is partly a character invented by the tradition. Authorship begins as attribution.
- **Live disputes kept on purpose:** 18 vs 21 tablets (two real counts, different dates); name vs office vs institution; token theory's reach (accounting-origin of writing broadly accepted; the complex-token-to-pictograph genealogy contested - see Michalowski's review, pointedly titled "Tokenism").

### Writerly details

- Palm-sized: 6.8 x 7.2 cm, 1.9 cm thick - roughly a square drinks coaster of baked clay, with slight cracks; press rounded it to "3 x 3 inches."
- Numbers were not drawn but pressed: rotate the reed stylus, push the round end into wet clay - deep circles and crescent moons. Words were scratched with the pointed end. Two gestures, punch and scratch, for two kinds of meaning.
- The period sign on MS 1717: a sun/day sign holding 3 x 10 + 7 - thirty-seven months. Three years and a month of barley on one small square of clay.
- 134,813 litres over 37 months is roughly 120 litres of barley a day, every day, for over three years.
- Two pictograms only recently deciphered: a brick structure (probably the brewery itself), and barley inside a vessel (beer) - per the Schøyen catalog.
- The modern provenance ledger: Basel chemists' collection, Christie's lot 48, bookseller Quaritch, Schøyen MS 1717, Bloomsbury hammer at GBP 140,000 to an anonymous American. A receipt that keeps generating receipts.
- In the Schøyen Collection the 5,000-year-old tablet lived in a green quarter-morocco gilt folding case made in 1997 by the bindery Aquarius of Barking, Essex.
- The archive is scattered like a dropped account book: Berlin, the British Museum, the Met, the Louvre, Schøyen - roughly 75 tablets and fragments, all apparently in one scribal hand.
- The tablets survived because they were garbage: used briefly, discarded, shoveled in as fill under the next rebuilding of the Eanna ("House of Heaven") precinct.
- Before tablets: thumb-sized clay tokens - cones, spheres, cylinders, ovoids - from c. 8000 BCE; later sealed inside hollow clay balls and impressed on the outside, so the envelope listed its own contents.
- Kushim's "name" is two signs, KU and ŠIM, tucked in a corner compartment. No birth, no death, no sentence. Eighteen receipts and a sum.
- Of the ~5,000 earliest written tablets, about 85 percent are accounting and the rest are word lists for training scribes; not one is a story, prayer, or letter.
- The Disk of Enheduanna: 25 cm across, 7 cm thick - dinner-plate diameter, doorstop heft - found smashed in the giparu at Ur in 1926; Enheduanna stands tallest in flounced robe and rolled conical cap, watching a priest pour a libation.

### Cautions

- Do NOT say the Kushim tablet is "in the Schøyen Collection in Oslo" - sold 8 July 2020 to an anonymous US collector. Guinness and many web sources are out of date.
- The Schøyen Collection is Oslo and London, Norway - not Sweden (a widely scraped summary says "Oslo, Sweden").
- Do not flatly call Kushim "the first named person in history": name vs office vs institution is unsettled. Hedge, or use the dispute itself.
- Avoid "signature" as literal fact - it is an account designation, not a signature in the modern sense.
- If quoting Harari's 29,086, flag the probable typo (28,086) - but keep the hedge; no published erratum exists.
- The tablets were NOT scientifically excavated; "from the Inanna Temple at Uruk" is an inference from content. Provenance humility required, especially near the 2021-22 seizure story (which the Kushim tablet was not part of - don't conflate).
- Kushim was an administrator (SANGA), not "a brewer"; the tablet is bookkeeping, not a beer recipe; it is clay, not stone.
- No wedges yet: Uruk-period signs are incised lines and pressed circles; canonical cuneiform wedges develop c. 2500 BCE onward.
- Don't claim proto-cuneiform "writes Sumerian"; the language is uncertain, the pronunciation reconstructed.
- Token theory: "writing began as accounting" is safe; the full token-to-sign genealogy is not consensus.
- Enheduanna's authorship carries a serious scholarly asterisk (all copies ~500 years late) - and the asterisk is the richer fact. Do NOT cite "Civil: 40%" (unverifiable).
- Keep the two firsts distinct: Kushim = earliest recorded personal name (maybe), c. 3100 BCE; Enheduanna = earliest named author of literature, c. 2300 BCE. ~800 years apart.
- Date discipline: Uruk period c. 3400-3000 BCE; Kushim tablets Uruk III, "31st century BCE" per Schøyen; writing's first appearance c. 3350 BCE. Avoid "5,500 years ago."

### Disputed / corrected during verification

### Writerly details

- A pale beige clay slab, 11.6 x 5.0 x 2.6 cm - "roughly the size of an iPhone" (Moshenska) - cracked across the face, apparently heated or burned at some point.
- The scribe used every surface: front, back, and the last lines wrapped onto the left edge. Roughly fifty lines of grievance.
- Its paper trail of names: U.16814a in the field; 1953,0411.71 on arrival in London; BM 131236; UET V 81 in print; P414985 in the database. Five names for one complaint.
- Ea-nasir's address: No. 1 Old Street - Woolley named Ur's excavated streets after Oxford's. A mud-brick house with its own chapel, later carved into smaller dwellings.
- The disputed sums: one mina of silver (~500 g) against 18 talents of copper (~540 kg), written 1(u) 8(disz) gu2 uruda.
- The letter's first word is a command to a third party: qibima, "speak!" - letters were dictated and voiced aloud at the far end; every letter is a script for someone else's mouth.
- The British Museum label is bureaucratic deadpan: "Complaint about delivery of the wrong grade of copper." It sat next to a case of copper artefacts.
- House F: 45 square metres of school where the name "tablet house" was literal - old tablets built into floors, walls, furniture; a recycling bin by the courtyard bench, itself built of whole tablets plastered over, holding fragments soaking back into fresh clay.
- Beginners' tablets were round like buns or lentils: the teacher's model, the pupil's wobblier copy beside it.
- The Hammurabi stele: 2.25 m of dark polished stone; the columns the Elamite king ground smooth for his own inscription were never inscribed - he made a blank and left it blank.
- The Nineveh curse is bibliographic: erase my name and write yours, and may the gods erase your name and your seed. The fire that destroyed the library preserved it.
- Meme ledger: Reddit, 25 February 2015, "1750 BC problems"; Tumblr the same day; the subreddit r/ReallyShittyCopper; the fans' word for visiting is "the pilgrimage."
- March 2021: thousands of tonnes of "copper" arrive in China as paving stones spray-painted copper. Tumblr: "Somehow, Ea-Nasir has returned."
- The BM's 3D scan: 959 downloads vs the Rosetta Stone's 10,000+. Fans 3D-print their own complaint tablets.

### Cautions

- Oppenheim's 1967 translation is copyrighted until January 1, 2063. Paraphrase or brief snippets only; Wikipedia removed the full text on copyright grounds. The free ISAC PDF is authorized, not public domain. And it is 1967, not 1976 (a circulating misdate).
- Do not say "found in 1922." The expedition ran 1922-34; the AH quarter was dug late; CDLI lists no stratigraphic context for this tablet. Safest: "found during Woolley's 1922-34 excavations."
- The "kept his hate mail as trophies" reading is meme folklore; Moshenska: "likely inaccurate." Routine archive, possibly awaiting recycling.
- The letter is Akkadian, not Sumerian; by 1750 BCE Sumerian was a school language. "5,000 years ago" overstates by more than a millennium (~3,770 years).
- "1,080 pounds" is Oppenheim's modernization; the tablet says 18 talents. Don't treat "pound" as Babylonian.
- Nanni disputes the debt - keep Oppenheim's "(?)".
- Copper was not mined in Dilmun; Dilmun was the entrepôt, Magan the source.
- Hammurabi's code is not the first law code (Ur-Nammu's is ~3 centuries older), and may not have functioned as applied statute law - court records rarely cite it; 50+ school copies show it lived as a classic.
- Museum-location drift: home is the British Museum, but 2025-27 it is in Abu Dhabi; the BM record currently says "Not on display."
- Guinness's "3,767 years old" was the age at certification; c. 1750 BCE is conventional (middle chronology) and approximate.
- "30,000" at Nineveh counts tablets AND fragments. And the Library of Alexandria did not burn down in one fire - do not import that myth into library-fire comparisons.

### Writerly details

- K.3375 is roughly the size of a paperback: 15.24 cm tall, a surviving upper-right corner, up to 51 lines per column.
- One face sealed under "a thick whitish lime-like deposit" that brushing could not shift; one man in the Museum permitted to clean it, and he was away for weeks (per Budge).
- Smith's first trade was engraving banknotes - apprenticed at 14 - before teaching himself cuneiform in lunch hours at the British Museum.
- Exactly one thousand guineas from a newspaper; the triumphant telegram ran on 21 May 1873 - garbled, Smith insisted, with the inserted phrase "as the season is closing."
- The "missing" fragment carries the museum prefix DT - for Daily Telegraph - number DT.42: seventeen lines that later proved to belong to a different flood entirely.
- Smith died of dysentery in Aleppo at 36; the Queen granted his widow £150 a year.
- For eighteen years (1872-1890) the hero wore the wrong name: Izdubar. The correction hangs on one anonymous Babylonian student's syllabic spelling, spotted by Pinches in 1890.
- Sin-leqi-unninni's name is itself a sentence: "O Sin, accept my prayer!" His credit appears only in a later catalogue - never on the poem's own tablets.
- Thompson's 1928 translation was published by Luzac & Co. at 46 Great Russell Street - directly across the road from the British Museum.
- Utnapishtim's wife marks the failed vigil in bread: one loaf per sleeping day set by his head - seven loaves in successive states of staleness, a calendar of sleep.
- Gilgamesh dives with stones tied to his feet "in the manner of Bahrein pearl-divers" (Thompson's gloss); the thorny plant is named, roughly, "the old man becomes young."
- The serpent is called an "earth-lion"; it sloughs its skin as it goes - the youth Gilgamesh lost, visibly worn by something else.
- The gods cower "like curs" in the flood and gather over the sacrifice "like flies"; the ark grounds on the mountain and holds fast six counted days before dove, swallow, raven.
- The library was preserved by its own destruction: the 612 BCE fire baked the clay harder and the falling palace buried it.
- In 2011 the epic was still growing: twenty new lines of Cedar Forest soundscape - birds, cicadas, screaming monkeys - three thousand years after the poem was "finished."

### Cautions

- The undressing anecdote rests entirely on Budge (1925), fifty years late, no witness named, narrator demonstrably unreliable elsewhere. Tell it as a story that is told - never as verified fact.
- Do not say Smith found "the missing piece of the Gilgamesh flood tablet": DT.42 is Atrahasis. He completed the Babylonian flood story, not Tablet XI.
- Do not put "a maggot dropped from his nostril" in quotes from a public-domain source - that wording is George's (1999). Thompson 1928 restores the broken line differently.
- "Six days and seven nights" - not "seven days and seven nights," which imports the biblical rhythm. The epic reuses the six/seven formula for grief, sleep test, and storm alike.
- Sin-leqi-unninni is not "the author" in the modern sense; attribution from a later catalogue, dates loose, contribution disputed.
- Tablet XII is an appendix inconsistent with Tablet VII; the poem proper ends at Tablet XI.
- The plant confers renewed youth, not immortality; the immortality test (staying awake) was already failed. Two failures, not one.
- Ashurbanipal's library: royal scholarly collection, not public; preserved BY burning - the structural opposite of the Alexandria story (which itself was not one fire).
- Names by tradition and era: Utnapishtim / Atra-hasis / Ziusudra; Smith's "Hasisadra" and "Izdubar." Use the right name for the right text; "Gilgamesh" as a reading dates only from 1890.
- Thompson 1928 quotes were verified against noisy OCR; collate with the page scans (archive.org: thompson-1928-gilgamesh) before print, and set one consistent policy for his parentheses and restoration marks.
- Mount Nisir (Thompson) = Nimush (current scholarship). Pick one, gloss the other.
- Smith's career dates between apprenticeship and 1872 (the 1861 sorting work; "Senior Assistant, early 1870") did not clear verification (truncated verdict) - re-check before print.
