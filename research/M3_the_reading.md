### Disputed / corrected during verification

- **CORRECTED — the Deja/Usenet timeline.** Original claim said Google's Feb 2001 Deja acquisition made 500M+ messages searchable "reaching back to 1981." Wrong: at acquisition the archive reached back only to March 1995. The 1981 depth (and the 700-million count) arrived 11 December 2001, when Google launched its "20 Year Usenet Archive" after adding the UTZoo tapes and other donated collections. The correction is itself a better story: even Google had to be handed the early years on old magnetic tape.
- **CORRECTED — the "sandwich" quote.** "If you're not Google or OpenAI or Microsoft, almost everybody is relying on Common Crawl" was attributed to Gil Elbaz, quoted in Mozilla's 2024 report. Doubly wrong: the sentence appears nowhere in the report, and the actual speaker is **Stefan Baack — the Mozilla researcher who wrote the report** — in a 96layers.ai interview (2 Apr 2024). Verbatim: "If you're not one of these big companies as far as I'm aware, almost everybody is relying on Common Crawl, which offers this kind of data for free." A misattribution about provenance, caught inside a dossier about provenance.
- **Precision: GeoCities torrent date.** The 652 GB salvage torrent was released 26 Oct 2010 — the first anniversary of the deletion — not in 2009 (the *downloading* was 2009). Don't use the early-press "900 GB" figure.
- **Precision: NYT link rot.** "Over half of articles" → 53% of articles containing *deep* links.
- **Precision: Internet Archive government pages.** 2004 is the start of the End of Term collaboration; the 400M+ pages were preserved since fall 2024.
- **Standing dispute: what counts as a "website."** The 1-billion-websites milestone counts hostnames (Netcraft); ~75% inactive. Kept as a definitional dispute, not a fact about living pages.
- **Standing dispute: how English the web is.** W3Techs says 49.2%; multilingualism researchers argue homepage-only sampling overcounts English. Present as "of measured website content."
- **Standing dispute: Wikipedia's gender skew.** 80–91% male depending on survey year and opt-in method. Direction undisputed; precision illusory.

### Writerly details

- The web was announced as a Usenet post — "Newsgroups: alt.hypertext / Date: 6 Aug 91 14:56:20 GMT" — signed off "Collaborators welcome!" The new medium introduced itself politely inside the old one it would swallow.
- A GeoCities address read like a postal address: www.geocities.com/Heartland/Hills/1234 — neighborhood, street, house number. Family pages in Heartland, UFO pages in Area51, code in SiliconValley; residents were "homesteaders"; 29 neighborhoods by 1996.
- Yahoo paid $3.57 billion in stock for GeoCities in 1999 and deleted it ten years later; volunteers' salvage of at least 38 million pages fits in one 652 GB torrent, released on the deletion's first anniversary — a whole suburb of the web, zipped, with a memorial date.
- GPT-2's definition of good prose: any link a Redditor shared that earned at least 3 karma. Three upvotes as the gate of the canon.
- GPT-3's diet: 45 terabytes of compressed Common Crawl boiled down to 570 gigabytes — roughly ninety-nine pages discarded for every page kept.
- Common Crawl, the quarry under most large language models, is run by a tiny nonprofit founded in 2007 by one man; Mozilla titled its report on this "Training Data for the Price of a Sandwich."
- Google's entire Usenet archive — 700+ million messages, two decades back to 1981, partly recovered from donated tapes — equals about ten minutes of WhatsApp traffic at 2020's 100-billion-a-day rate.
- Technorati's 2006 ticker: two new blogs every second, 175,000 a day, 1.6 million posts daily; the 50-millionth blog logged 31 July 2006; the blogosphere doubling every 200 days.
- Wikipedia adds ~11 MB of text a day — its own statisticians call it "eleven book volumes a day" — and absorbs more than two edits every second. Its 7-millionth English article (28 May 2025) was "Operators and Things."
- The Wayback Machine's trillionth page arrived October 2025: well over 99 petabytes of web saved inside a former Christian Science church at 300 Funston Avenue, San Francisco.
- In the church sanctuary stand 100+ three-foot statues — every employee who serves three years gets one — a self-aware echo of the terracotta army guarding an emperor's tomb.
- Radicati's estimated 361.6 billion emails a day works out to ~45 per living human, most written by no one: receipts, resets, reminders.
- Of the hyperlinks the New York Times published in 1998, 72% now lead nowhere; in Supreme Court opinions, 49.9% of cited links no longer show what the justices saw.
- English is 49.2% of measured website content and ~45% of Common Crawl, written disproportionately by young men: Reddit's news users 71% male and 59% under thirty; Wikipedia's editors 90% male in the 2011 survey, ~80% in recent ones.

### Cautions

- Don't say "the first website appeared on August 6, 1991." info.cern.ch ran from late 1990; 6 Aug 1991 is the public Usenet announcement. Pick the precise event.
- "One billion websites" (Sept 2014) counts hostnames, mostly parked/inactive; the count dipped back under a billion afterward. Never "a billion active websites."
- GeoCities was not totally annihilated: Archive Team, Internet Archive, ReoCities, OoCities saved large portions; Yahoo Japan's GeoCities survived until March 2019. Honest phrasing: mass deletion with partial volunteer rescue.
- Torrent (652 GB) ≠ all of GeoCities (~10 TB estimate). Salvage ≠ the whole. Torrent released 2010, not 2009.
- Harvard 2014's 49.9% covers link rot AND reference rot (page exists, content drifted). "Half the links are dead" overstates dead links specifically.
- Radicati email figures: commercial projections including machine traffic. Always "estimated."
- Common Crawl is a sample (~2.5–2.8B pages/month, robots.txt-respecting, English-skewed). Never "trained on the entire internet."
- Keep WebText (GPT-2, Reddit-derived) distinct from WebText2 (GPT-3 component) and OpenWebText (third-party replication). GPT-3 used a filtered 570 GB slice at 60% weight, not the whole archive.
- Wikipedia editor gender: attribute "90%" to the 2011 survey; ~80% in recent Community Insights. All opt-in surveys.
- ITU 5.5B users (68%, 2024) was revised to ~5.8B (71%) in the 2025 release. Date any figure.
- Usenet (1979–80) predates the web, not the internet (ARPANET 1969, email early 1970s). Not "the first network writing."
- Google's Usenet archive is famously incomplete and poorly searchable today; pre-1981 years essentially gone. Feb 2001 = 500M messages to 1995; Dec 2001 = 700M to 1981.
- Wayback's "one trillion pages" counts captures/snapshots (URLs at moments in time), not distinct sites or documents.
- W3Techs' 49% English measures homepages of the top million sites — "of measured website content," not "of everything written online."

- The Examiner's readers met the sonnet with its weak line still in it — "Yet could I never judge what men could mean" — and Hunt, printing it, teased its "one incorrect rhyme." The famous "pure serene" exists only in the 1817 book: even the testimony of wonder got rewritten between printings. (Verify Hunt's exact sentence against the Examiner facsimile before quoting it.)
- The sonnet's record of the night contains an error ("stout Cortez" for Balboa) that was never corrected; the correction, when it came, was a parenthetical "(A.T.)" in someone else's anthology — circulating by December 1861, the same year Clarke first told the story, forty-five years after the night itself. The mistake was kept; the poem is about the feeling, and the feeling was accurate.
- The paper Clarke found at breakfast is apparently lost (the Harvard sheet was likely the Severn copy, per Barnard, paraphrase only). The original record of the transformative night does not survive — only one man's memory of it, first printed 45 years on.
- Physical relay: parted "at day-spring," the Borough to Clerkenwell, "may be, two miles," paper on the breakfast table "by ten o'clock," "a letter with no other enclosure than his famous sonnet."
- Keats's own directions to his lodging (letter quoted by Clarke): "run the gauntlet over London Bridge... knock at my door, which is nearly opposite a meeting."
- Adjacent but separate scene (1878 p. 126, schoolboy-era Spenser, NOT the Chapman night): "what an image that is — 'sea-shouldering whales!'"
- Pepys was in the rain because his house was flooding: "my house was all afloat, and I in all the rain up to the gutters." A man on his own roofline at night in a thunderstorm, unstopping gutters. The rain survives only because he wrote it down; the water itself is 363 years gone.
- "Dabbled" is the load-bearing word: a child's water-play verb for half an hour of cold storm "enough to have killed a man."
- The domestic frame: that same day he and his wife carried "several heavy things... up and down stairs" to move their bedroom ("to lie above"); he had taken two pills and "kept the house"; afterwards he changed "from top to toe" and went to bed "doubtful of my doing myself hurt." (The bad ear and cold are the 28th's entry; next morning, the 30th, Mr. Holliard "searched my ear.")
- The date is Michaelmas Day, and he never says so.
- The Wordsworth backup literally enacts the chapter's theme: rain, the old postman ("our patient bow-bent friend"), Coleridge's letter taken "to my bosom,—a safer place for it" at the top of the White Moss. Rain, letter, keeping — words carried home through weather.

### Cautions

1. **Single witness, late memory.** The entire Chapman night rests on Clarke alone, first printed 45 years after the event (1861), revised at ~58 years (1878). No second witness or contemporary document of the night exists. Credit it as Clarke's telling, not neutral fact.
2. **"Read until daylight" is an inference.** The only dawn evidence is "We had parted, as I have already said, at day-spring" — and the back-reference has NO antecedent in either printing (verified by grep of both scans: "parted" and "day-spring" occur exactly once each, at the back-reference itself). Do not stage a described all-night scene as Clarke's; the dawn lives in six words.
3. **Not Clarke's phrases:** "shouting with delight at passages of special energy" (later biographical paraphrase) and "scrap of paper" (he says "a letter with no other enclosure than his famous sonnet"). Do not quote either as Clarke.
4. **The 9 October 1816 letter date is editorial** (Rollins convention; the letter bore only the weekday, no postmark). Say "dated by its editors to 9 October 1816." Clarke's two printings even quote the letter's directions differently (1861: "first turning to the left, and then the first to the right"; 1878: "the first turning to the right").
5. **Quote one Clarke printing and cite it** — the 1878 is canonical; the two tellings drift. The 1874 *Gentleman's Magazine* intermediate version is unverified.
6. **They read both epics**, not "the Odyssey"; avoid Wikipedia's framing.
7. **Never present the 1817 sonnet text as what the Examiner printed.** The Examiner text differs materially throughout (lines 5, 7, spelling, punctuation, "CORTEZ" in capitals). Pick the printing the scene requires and quote that page. Colvin's "Octr 1816" is Colvin's, not the page's.
8. **Golden Treasury:** quote the verified 1861 page-image wording, not Bartleby; wording, numbering, capitalization, and the "Balbóa" accent all vary by edition. A.T. = Tennyson is standard but unattested in a primary source — phrase as "initials standardly identified as Tennyson's."
9. **Typesetting check survives partially resolved:** the 1817 sonnet's dashes and apostrophes are now verified against the page image (the djvu OCR is garbage — "wild surprise," dropped dashes). Still open: the 1878 italics of "The sea had soak'd his heart through" (OCR artifact "soaJc'd" implies italic type) — check the page image before reproducing the emphasis.
10. **Em-dashes inside verbatim PD quotes** (the sonnet's "Pacific—and," Knight's joining dash, "bosom,—a safer place") collide with the STYLE_BIBLE §5 ban unless quoted matter is exempt — settle that before the chapter prints any of them.
11. **Pepys edition discipline:** quote the Bright/Wheatley 1893 text ONLY. The Latham & Matthews transcription (1970–83) is IN COPYRIGHT and differs; never mix wordings. "Lighten" and "closett" are correct Wheatley spellings, verified against the 1893 print.
12. **Wheatley prints no weekday.** "Tuesday" rests on calendar arithmetic (independently reproduced) plus the pepysdiary.com entry title (site 403s direct fetches; title via search index). Two independent legs — keep both cited; neither is the printed page.
13. **OS/NS:** give 29 September 1663 (the date Pepys wrote); it is Old Style, = 9 October NS. Flag silently or in notes.
14. **Michaelmas spot-check trap:** the diary text never mentions the feast, but pepysdiary.com's annotations do — anyone checking that page may think the dossier erred. The claim is about the diary text.
15. **Wordsworth edition discipline:** Knight (1897) modernized and silently cut; quote his wording exactly or not at all. Pagination pp. 87–88 verified in the 1904 reprint; 1897 first-edition pagination assumed identical, not verified — cite by entry date, which is stable.
