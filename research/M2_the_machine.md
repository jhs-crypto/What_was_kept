### Disputed / corrected during verification

- **Aldine Virgil print run (CORRECTED).** The dossier's "around 4,000 copies" was single-sourced to one Oxford project page and could not be independently corroborated; Aldine scholarship generally estimates 1,000-3,000. The correction wins: attribute the 4,000 explicitly as one outlier estimate, or omit.
- **Mallinckrodt pamphlet (CORRECTED).** Title is *De ortu ac progressu...dissertatio historica* ("ac", not "et"); sources split 1639 vs 1640 — write "published in Cologne in 1639-40 for the bicentenary of printing."
- **British Library donor (CORRECTED).** "Thomas Greville" was a garble; the vellum copy came from book collector Thomas Grenville (1755-1846), whose library was bequeathed to the British Museum. A "Greville" misspelling circulates online — that is the contamination source.
- **Gutenberg print run (HONEST DISPUTE — keep).** The only contemporary figures (158 vs 180) contradict each other inside a single letter. The first great print run is itself a rumor. Modern 160-185 is reconstruction.
- **Survivor counts (HONEST DISPUTE — keep).** 47/48/49 substantial, 20/21 complete, depending on criteria. Verification adds: a "25 complete" figure circulates in tertiary sources — avoid it; stick to 20-21 from major holding libraries.
- **Luther's market share (HONEST DISPUTE — keep).** A third vs a fifth, depending on whether you count titles, editions, or copies.
- **Helmasperger total (hedged on verification).** Britannica "probably totaling 2,020 guilders"; historyofinformation 2,026. Write "about 2,020" or "2,020-2,026 by modern computation"; never an exact documented sum.
- **Frankfurt 1454 (precision from verification).** Piccolomini was at the Imperial Diet of Frankfurt (Oct 1454) — do NOT say "trade fair," as popular accounts do.

### Writerly details

- A full Royal sheet ~42 x 60 cm; folded once, each untrimmed leaf ~42 x 30 cm — 643 to a complete Bible, 324 in vol I, 319 in vol II.
- ~290 distinct sorts in the type case — the metal had to counterfeit every contraction a scribe's hand would make.
- ~5,200 pieces of cast type locked in the press per sheet, while another 5,200 were being set and a third batch redistributed.
- One vellum Bible: an estimated 170 calfskins.
- Cremer dated his finishing notes by saints' days: the Assumption, then St Bartholomew's Day, 1456.
- The Fust loans: 800 gulden at 6% in 1450, another 800 in 1452; compound total ~2,020-2,026 by Nov 1455; the notary was clerk of the bishopric of Bamberg.
- The Keio copy still carries four of its original ~30 leather "index buttons" — finding tabs sewn to leaf edges in fifteenth-century Mainz; it crossed the Pacific on a $4.9M hammer.
- Aldus's device, first used 1502: dolphin twined round an anchor, *Festina lente* — Erasmus glossed the dolphin as nimbleness anchored by accuracy.
- The 1501 Virgil's italic was Griffo's; the octavo meant Virgil in one hand.
- Carolus had printed twelve weekly issues before he even petitioned the council.
- The *Daily Courant*: one page, foreign news front, ads back, beside a tavern at Fleet Bridge; Mallet sold within ~40 days.
- By 1750 Dutch literacy ~85%, Spain's ~8 — the corpus pooled where the rain fell.
- Buringh & van Zanden's totals climb like a tide table: 12.6M by 1500 ... 641M by 1800.
- Google's 2010 census: ~a billion raw records from 150+ providers, deduplicated into "tomes," hardcover and paperback deliberately counted twice.

### Cautions

- Gutenberg did not invent printing or movable type: Bi Sheng's ceramic type (Song China, c. 1040s); the Korean *Jikji* (1377) is the oldest extant book printed with movable metal type. Gutenberg industrialized it for the Latin alphabet (punch-matrix-mould, oil ink, press).
- The B42 was not Gutenberg's first printing — indulgences, Donatus fragments, the Sibyllenbuch precede it. First major typographic book, not first printed object.
- Never a flat "180 copies." Never an exact survivor count without stating criteria.
- Luther's hammer at the Wittenberg door (31 Oct 1517) is disputed (Iserloh 1961): no eyewitness account; he verifiably mailed the theses to his bishops that day. The printing explosion is documented; the hammer is not.
- Luther's "Printing is God's highest and ultimate gift" is Table Talk — students' after-dinner notes, uneven reliability. Attribute to the Table Talk, not his pen.
- Aldus did not "invent italics" — Griffo cut the type (and quarreled bitterly over credit). The Aldus-invented-the-semicolon claim (Bembo's *De Aetna*, 1496) is unverified — check before use.
- The 1501 incunabula cutoff is Mallinckrodt's arbitrary inheritance — nothing changed in printing on 1 January 1501.
- Pre-1800 literacy figures are inferences from signatures and book consumption; "could sign" understates reading, especially for women, often taught to read but not write.
- Carolus's *Relation* is "first newspaper" only under specific criteria (printed, periodical, public, current); handwritten avvisi are older; canonical date was 1609 until 2005 — even firsts get re-typeset.
- Google's 129,864,880 was an engineering estimate with a sense of humor: excluded serials and microforms, counted bindings separately. Quote with its date (Aug 2010) and its irony, not as a constant.
- Gutenberg was not "destroyed" by the lawsuit: in 1465 Archbishop Adolf II of Nassau reportedly granted him a courtier's pension (clothing, grain, wine, tax exemption). **UNVERIFIED THIS SESSION — re-verify against a primary source before use.** Avoid "died penniless and forgotten" either way.
- Britannica calls it a "three-volume work"; most copies are bound in two (some three or four) — binding was the buyer's choice; volume counts describe copies, not the edition.

### Disputed / corrected during verification

- **Stepped reckoner rediscovery (CORRECTED).** Not just 1876-vs-1879: the official Hannover account says 1894, in the Göttingen University Church attic. Date AND building disputed. The charming leaking-roof detail should not be pinned to a year.
- **Binary paper (CORRECTED, twice).** "Fohy," not "Fuxi," in the printed title; and the paper appeared in the Mémoires for 1703, printed 1705 — April 1703 is the date of receipt of Bouvet's letter, not of publication.
- **Bouvet transit (CORRECTED).** 4 Nov 1701 to 1 Apr 1703 is seventeen months, not fourteen. (The dossier's dates were right; its arithmetic wasn't. Even dossiers have transition probabilities.)
- **Turin (CORRECTED).** September 1840 (Congress of Italian Scientists, 16-30 Sept), not August. Babbage's mid-August departure from England seeded the error.
- **Note G authorship (HONEST DISPUTE — keep).** Bromley/Stein minimize her mathematics; defenders point to the 1843 correspondence, her catching Babbage's "grave mistake," and the published elaboration and philosophy being hers. The book should stage the dispute, not adjudicate it.
- **Royal Society demo day (hedge from verification).** 1 Feb 1673 NS = 22 Jan 1672/3 OS in Birch's minutes — hedge if printing the exact day.
- **Calculemus title (hedge from verification).** Gerhardt VII 200 is solid (Russell confirms); the Latin piece-title is editorial.

### Writerly details

- The surviving stepped reckoner: ~67 cm of polished brass and steel in an oak case, Hanover; restored 1894-96 by a calculator manufacturer.
- A thinking machine misplaced in an attic for a century, the search occasioned by a leaking roof (year and building disputed: 1876-1894).
- The medallion: silver, IMAGO CREATIONIS, designed in a letter, never struck in his lifetime; the duke chose wax seals instead.
- Bouvet's hexagram diagram left Beijing 4 Nov 1701 and reached Hanover 1 Apr 1703 — binary's "confirmation" from ancient China traveled seventeen months by ship and caravan.
- Babbage's silk Jacquard portrait: ~24,000 punched cards, over eight hours of weaving; soirée guests mistook silk for engraving.
- £17,500 of Treasury money — twenty-two brand-new Stephenson locomotives at 1831 prices — and no finished engine.
- The planned store: 1,000 numbers of 40 decimal digits — a memory of brass columns and toothed wheels.

- Note G reads "originate any thing" (two words) — silently modernizing is a small infidelity in a book about exact texts.

### Writerly details

- Markov's method was physical: 20,000 letters of Pushkin copied in pen onto drafting paper, 200 squares of 10x10, vowels counted column by column, columns paired off, tallies in forty small tables — days of pencil work by a 56-year-old who professed indifference to applications.
- Independence would predict ~3,700 double vowels; Pushkin yields 1,104. A vowel follows a vowel 12.8% of the time, a consonant 66.3%.
- In 1921 Markov told the Academy he could not attend for want of decent footwear; the boot committee was chaired by Maxim Gorky; Markov complained the boots didn't fit and were "stupidly stitched."
- Shannon's ladder runs from "XFOML RXKHRJFFJUJ" up to "THE HEAD AND IN FRONTAL ATTACK ON AN ENGLISH WRITER" — 27 symbols, 26 letters and the space.
- He made random English by hand: a book of random numbers, a 1939 cipher-history's frequency tables, a 1923 Harvard word count — and for higher orders, opening a book at random and reading until he found the letters just produced.
- Tukey's "bit" arrives amid desk hardware: a relay stores one bit; "a digit wheel on a desk computing machine has ten stable positions."
- The 1951 game text was *Jefferson the Virginian* — a Pulitzer-winning biography pulled off the shelf; the worked sample: "THE ROOM WAS NOT VERY LIGHT A SMALL OBLONG READING LAMP ON THE DESK SHED GLOW ON POLISHED WOOD BUT LESS ON THE SHABBY RED CARPET" — 129 symbols, 89 right.
- To prove the reduced text carries the same information, Shannon conjures "an identical twin of the individual who produced the sequence" — mathematically, not just biologically identical.
- Betty's only trace in the paper: "The writer is indebted to Mrs. Mary E. Shannon..."
- At 100 letters of context: 0.6-1.3 bits per letter, ~75% redundancy — three of every four letters of English are, statistically, already written for you.
- Turing's furniture: three people, separate room, answers typewritten or repeated by intermediary; the machine needs about 10^9 of storage — roughly 125 megabytes.
- The founding text of machine-can't-originate, slightly miscopied (one word dropped, one year wrong) by the founder of machine intelligence.
- Hollymeade, 43 Adlington Road, Wilmslow: the apple never tested; in the tiny spare room, a gold-electroplating rig dissolving gold in potassium cyanide; Woking crematorium, three mourners. The executors later changed the house's name on the deeds.
- Coroner Ferns: "In a man of his type, one never knows what his mental processes are going to do next."
- Calendar texture for 1913: Markov lectured on 23 January by Russia's Julian calendar, in the year the tsar celebrated 300 years of Romanovs and Markov celebrated 200 years of the law of large numbers.
