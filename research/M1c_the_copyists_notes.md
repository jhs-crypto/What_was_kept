### Writerly details

- Seized scrolls were shelved under the accession label "from the ships" (ek ploion): the provenance field of a confiscation, preserved in Galen - and tell it as Galen's story.
- Fifteen talents of silver: the deposit Ptolemy III forfeited to keep Athens's official tragedy scrolls; Athens kept the silver and received copies on the finest writing material. A library acquiring originals by losing money on purpose (Galen's story, again).
- Didymus's father sold fish (Suda); the son became Bronze-Guts, then Book-Forgetter; convicted of contradiction by his own book, fetched and opened in front of him (Quintilian).
- Of Didymus's 3,500 or 4,000 books (the count itself is unstable between sources), what survives is one provincial roll from Hermoupolis, P.Berol. inv. 9780, copied around a century after his death, its back later reused for a Stoic ethics handbook; published Berlin, 1904.
- The Pinakes: 120 book-rolls of bibliography, with biographies, incipits, and line counts; the catalog of everything kept, itself not kept.
- A granite block found in Alexandria in 1847, now in Vienna: a hollow 19.5 by 23 cm, inscribed "Dioskourides, 3 rolls." Drawn for 150 years as Library shelving; Bagnall concluded it was a statue base. A half-million-roll library would have needed 166,667 of them - the book's own arithmetic, and say so.
- Timon of Phlius, c. 230 BCE, on the Mouseion's salaried scholars: well-propped pedants quarreling "in the Muses' bird-cage" - the first joke about academia, made about the first research institute.
- Strabo walked the palace quarter c. 25-20 BCE and itemized the Mouseion's walk, seating, mess-hall, and priest-director, but no library building: the most famous library in history is invisible in the best eyewitness description of its own campus.
- Ptolemy VIII "Benefactor," re-nicknamed Kakergetes ("Malefactor"), scattered philologians, geometers, musicians, painters, trainers, and physicians across the Aegean, where poverty forced them to teach: the persecution accidentally seeded Greek learning everywhere - "the Alexandrians were the teachers of all Greeks and barbarians" (the exiles, not the city).
- After 145 BCE the successor of Zenodotus, Eratosthenes, and Aristarchus was Cydas, "from the Spear-bearers": a palace soldier shelved into the scholars' chair.
- The Bellum Alexandrinum, written on Caesar's own side, claims Alexandria's buildings were vaulted, timberless, "secure from fire": the earliest source closest to the fire denies the city could burn.
- Domitian, restocking Rome's burned libraries c. 90 CE, sent scribes TO Alexandria to transcribe and correct (Suetonius): the library as something you copy from, 140 years after it supposedly burned.
- Ammianus described the Serapeum's libraries in the past tense (fuerunt) while the building still stood; Orosius, c. 416, saw the book chests themselves, standing empty.
- Reynolds & Wilson's citation for papyrus lifespan is itself Galen (18(2).630 Kühn): a roll might last 300 years; the average was far less; Alexandria's humid sea air has preserved not one ancient papyrus to the present day.
- The first dated minuscule book in the world: the Uspensky Gospels, AD 835. Nearly all surviving classical Greek descends from copies made in that script in that century; the uncial exemplar was then - generally, with exceptions - discarded: the old copy thrown away by the act that saved the text.
- Ibn al-Qifti's tale (c. 1227): the scrolls allegedly heated Alexandria's bathhouses for six months. (The famous extra twist - that he forgot the number of bathhouses inside his own story - is UNVERIFIED in any scholarly translation; do not build on it.)
- The Suda's verb for staging a play is "to teach" it (edidaxe): Sophocles "taught" 123 plays, drilled into choruses.
- The filing accident, exactly: the alphabetic Euripides plays run from partway through epsilon to kappa - Helen; Electra, Heracles, Heraclidae; Suppliants, Ion, the two Iphigenias; Cyclops. Snell pictured two roll-cases, five rolls each: someone kept the E-to-K box.
- The straw dot: a fleck of straw embedded in L's paper at Helen line 95 was copied as if it were ink by the scribe of P, the parchment deluxe copy. That is how Zuntz proved the cheap working copy is the parent and the luxury volume the child.
- Triclinius revised L over years in inks of different colors; because P was copied between his campaigns, the ink color dates his thoughts.
- Bacchae survives in L only to line 755; it stood last in the collection, and the back pages of a codex die first. The end of the play is a reconstructed ruin.
- Two old books in the same Florentine case (pluteo 32): the mid-10th-c. parchment codex 32.9, the oldest copy of Aeschylus's and Sophocles's surviving plays, and the paper codex 32.2, the only witness to the nine alphabetic Euripides plays. Phrase as "the oldest copy of," never "the only place they live" - and never call both "L."
- Sappho's Book 1 ends with a librarian's stock count: a colophon reading 1,320 lines (330 stanzas). The only hard number in the whole Sappho arithmetic; the famous "10,000 lines" is a modern extrapolation from it.
- Fr. 31 survives inside On the Sublime, a treatise whose own author is unknown and whose own text is incomplete. The ark is itself a fragment, and anonymous.
- The oldest Sappho (the Cologne papyrus, early 3rd c. BCE, within ~300 years of her) was recycled as mummy cartonnage: she survived as packaging.
- The Ichneutae roll came out of the Oxyrhynchus rubbish mounds: 400+ lines of satyrs sniffing after Apollo's stolen cattle, published 1912 after two millennia in a dump.
- Dyskolos: ~969 verses, only ~9 wholly lost; published 1958, the first new complete-ish Greek comedy in over two thousand years; the codex sandwiched it between Samia and Aspis.
- Livy's fifth surviving pentad (books 41-45) depends on one manuscript of about 500 CE in Vienna, holes and all; and for 105 of his 107 lost books, the table-of-contents survives while the books do not. Only books 136 and 137 are nothing at all.
- Date symmetry, if wanted: the Brothers Poem was, in West's phrase, the greatest Sappho find "for 92 years" - and 92 is also the higher Suda count for Euripides' plays. (Attribute the phrase; the symmetry is the book's own observation.)
- The St Gall Priscian is a big book: 39 x 28.5 cm, 249 pages of Latin grammar, copied in Ireland c. 845-851 and carried to the Continent; the Viking quatrain sits in an upper margin (whose hand wrote it is inference, not catalogue fact).
- The hangover note is written in Ogham, the old strokes-on-a-line alphabet, twice (pp. 189a, 204b): a 9th-century scribe encrypting his hangover in a dead script inside a Latin grammar. Plummer: "It is to be feared that the good scribe had made too free overnight."
- The margins call on helpers by name between complaints: "faue brigita," "sancta brigita adiuua scriptorem istius artis," "in nomine almi Patricii," and once simply Fergus, whose soul gets a blessing right before "I am very cold."
- Time stamps in the margins: "tertia hora" (p. 212b), "medon lai" (midday, p. 231a), "tempus est prandii" (lunchtime, p. 228), "nox ades" (night is here, p. 246a): a working day reconstructible from one grammar book's edges.
- Cluniac monks under the silence rule signed for a book by miming the turning of leaves; a psalter was hands-crowned-on-head for King David; a pagan classic added an ear-scratch, like a dog (Madan's account; the rule's primary source is unnamed).
- Cassiodorus fitted Vivarium with self-feeding lamps, a sundial for sun and a water clock for cloud, so the copying would not stop at night; in his ninety-second year he wrote his monks a spelling primer because, by their own complaint, they could not get the ancients' thoughts down correctly.
- Benedict's Lent rule: one book per monk per year, "read straight through from the beginning," with seniors patrolling for idlers; the Rule's only writing equipment appears in a list of things a monk may NOT own: book, tablets, pen.
- Florentius of Valeránica finished his Moralia on 11 April 945, aged about twenty-four or twenty-five, and built into it a labyrinth page, a letter-grid reading "Florentium indignum memorare" along every path (one enthusiast computed 354,200 routes - hedge the number).
- Fifteen years later, in the León Bible's great Omega, Florentius and his pupil Sanctius are painted raising cups to one another at the moment of completion: the drink the colophons beg for, made visible at the alphabet's last letter.
- The Tábara Beatus's tower miniature (f. 171v): a stone bell-tower with the writing room attached, two figures at one table with pens, another cutting parchment - often described as the earliest surviving depiction of a medieval scriptorium. Magius died 30 October 968 with the book half-done; Emeterius finished it in three months, dated 27 July 970 by the Spanish Era, a calendar running 38 years ahead.
- A Trinity College Dublin law manuscript carries Hugh Mac Egan's colophon of Christmas 1350, written in the plague's second year in Ireland, asking each reader for one paternoster; a note a year later records, in effect, that he was still alive. (His exact age and the exact night are disputed between translations - hedge.)
- The order of operations in 1229: unbind, scrape, wash, cut, fold, rotate. Each Archimedes leaf folded in half made two prayer-book leaves, so the mathematics lies sideways under the prayers - a literal cross-grain of texts, the diagrams bisected by the fold.
- The prayer book is small - "not much larger than a hardback novel" (Smithsonian) - made from a large-format mathematics book.
- Seven books went into one: a mathematician, an orator, a philosopher's commentator, a Neoplatonist, a service book, two books still undeciphered - and the scribe kept none of their names.
- The scribe's own name, Ioannes Myronas, surfaced nearly 800 years later in an X-ray fluorescence scan, under a moldy 20th-century forged painting of St. John: a forged evangelist sitting on a real signature. April 1229 falls just before Easter - a working priest finishing his service book for the feast.
- Heiberg in 1906: a magnifying glass, natural light, and a camera, in the Metochion's library; he read most of it that way and published in Hermes the next year. The famous "huge day for science" was arguably 1907, not 1998.
- The auction lasted minutes on 29 October 1998, 2 p.m., Christie's New York: hammer at $2.0 million; the buyer ever after just "Mr. B." He walked the book in to the Walters himself in January 1999; Abigail Quandt then spent about four years taking it apart (the "one page every 15 days" average is single-sourced - hedge or drop).
- At SLAC's beamline 6-2, a synchrotron X-ray beam made the iron in tenth-century ink fluoresce: the dead ink literally glowed back.
- The full dataset went free online at 2 p.m. on 29 October 2008 - ten years to the day, almost to the hour, after the sale - under a Creative Commons Attribution license: the privately owned book made publicly readable.
- Tischendorf, who deciphered the Ephraemi palimpsest in Paris in the 1840s, also pocketed one leaf of the Archimedes palimpsest in Constantinople in the same decade; Cambridge bought it from his estate in 1876, and nobody knew what it was until 1968.
- In October 2025 a lost leaf of the palimpsest resurfaced in a museum in Blois, found by a researcher searching online: Sphere and Cylinder under a forged Daniel-in-the-lions'-den. The book was still giving up leaves while this dossier was being compiled. (LIVE - re-verify before print.)
- Scale of the economics: Codex Amiatinus weighs ~75 pounds and cost an estimated ~515 animal skins; Ceolfrith's three bibles, an estimated 1,500-plus; a vellum Gutenberg Bible, ~170 calfskins, estimated. A big book was a herd. Seven old books were cheaper than one new flock.

### Cautions

- **Do NOT stage a single great fire as the death of the Library of Alexandria.** The scholarly consensus (Bagnall 2002 is the standard statement) is decline by neglect, decay, and failure to recopy. Caesar's fire demonstrably did not end the institution.
- **Never print one scroll count as "the" size.** Every figure from 40,000 to 700,000 is contested, late, or manuscript-corrupted. The spread itself is the fact; if a working figure is unavoidable, "tens of thousands" with attribution to the dispute.
- **The Galen anecdotes** (ship seizures; the fifteen-talent trick) are single-sourced, ~450 years late, "probably unbelievable" (Bagnall). Tell them as Galen's stories. And the attalus English is one modern translation of a text with a partly Arabic transmission - tell the stories, don't quote that English as "Galen's words."
- **Founding-narrative trap:** Demetrius of Phaleron founding the Library for Ptolemy II is chronologically impossible; the earliest source is pseudonymous propaganda.
- **Date traps:** Ptolemy VIII's purge 145-144 BCE (Athenaeus says "seventh Ptolemy"; modern numbering VIII - do not silently harmonize). Aurelian's Bruchion destruction c. 272-273 CE - no ancient source names the Library in it. Serapeum 391, under Theophilus - "the mob destroyed the daughter library's books" goes beyond every primary source; none mentions books. Eunapius is "of Sardis."
- **Hypatia (murdered 415) had nothing to do with either library's destruction**; avoid the Sagan/Cosmos conflation entirely.
- **The Caliph Umar bathhouse story:** first attested c. 1203-1227, rejected by scholarship since Butler (1902). The famous "if they agree with the Book of God..." verdict circulates in Gibbon's wording, which was NOT verified verbatim - mark UNVERIFIED if quoted. The "I have forgotten the number of baths" line is translation-unstable and UNVERIFIED.
- **"Seneca says 40,000" carries an asterisk:** editors emend to 400,000 on Orosius's authority, and Orosius's own MSS split. If using the corruption-of-numbers irony, cite Bagnall pp. 351-52.
- **Plutarch is the FIRST source (c. 100-120 CE) to say the fire destroyed "the great library."** Seneca/Livy, a century closer, say only that 40,000 libri burned. Dio's translators split (Cary's Loeb names the library). Keep the warehouse-vs-Library question open; resolving it would falsify the evidence.
- **Strabo in English:** the LacusCurtius text is Jones's Loeb (1932), in copyright until 2028; for PD English use Hamilton & Falconer (1854-57) and verify their exact wording first.
- **"Zenodotus, appointed 284 BC"** is popular varnish; the Suda gives no year. Say "under the first two Ptolemies, prominent around 280 BCE." The Mouseion and the Library are not interchangeable: the community is well attested; the building is never described by any eyewitness.
- **Tragedian number discipline:** Sophocles "123 by the Suda's count" (the Suda itself hedges), never a bare 123. Do NOT average Aeschylus's three ancient totals (90 / 73 titles / seventy dramas + ~5 satyr plays). Do not average victory counts either. Prometheus Bound: live dispute, never "proved spurious." Euripides: "eighteen, nineteen if you count the Rhesus that is probably not his." The selection was a filter, not a committee meeting.
